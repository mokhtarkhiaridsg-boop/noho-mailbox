# Investor + Acquisition Prep

The asset library you need ready BEFORE you decide to raise capital
or sell. Most founders prep this in panic when an opportunity
arises; the good ones prep it slowly and have it ready.

**Principle**: An investor / acquirer due diligence runs faster and
yields better terms when your data room is clean. Build it as you
operate, not when you need it.

---

## When this matters

You&apos;ll use this asset library when:

1. **Raising venture capital** (Series A or later)
2. **Raising debt** (revenue-based financing, SBA loan, etc.)
3. **Strategic acquisition** (a competitor or adjacent player wants
   to buy you)
4. **Private equity recapitalization** (selling 51%+ to a PE firm)
5. **Earnout / partial exit** (selling a portion of equity to
   employees, family office, etc.)

For most service-business + SaaS combinations like ours, **#3 (strategic
acquisition)** is most common. CMRA SaaS acquirers might include:
iPostal1 / Anytime Mailbox, larger postal-services holdings, PE firms
in the small-business-services space.

## The standard data room

This is what acquirers / investors expect to see. Build it
incrementally; don&apos;t panic-build at the last minute.

### Section 1: Corporate

- Articles of Organization (LLC formation doc)
- Operating Agreement
- Members / shareholders ledger
- All annual filings (Statement of Information, etc.)
- Any minutes of major decisions (board / member meetings)
- All issued employee equity / stock option grants (if applicable)
- All cap table updates (signed)
- Beneficial Ownership Information (FinCEN BOI report — required
  since 2024)

### Section 2: Financial

- Last 3 years of P&L statements (audited if possible — required for
  Series A+)
- Last 3 years of balance sheets
- Last 3 years of cash flow statements
- Monthly P&L for the last 12 months (more granular)
- Federal tax returns (last 3 years)
- State tax returns (last 3 years)
- Sales tax filings (if applicable)
- All bank statements (last 12 months)
- All Stripe / payment processor reports
- AR aging report
- AP aging report
- Detailed customer-level revenue breakdown (top 20 customers)
- Cohort retention analysis

### Section 3: Customers + revenue

- Total active customer count by product line
- MRR by month for the last 24 months
- Churn rate by month
- LTV / CAC by acquisition channel
- Top 20 customers by revenue (with start dates + status)
- Customer concentration analysis (% of revenue from top 1, top 5,
  top 10)
- Customer testimonials / case studies
- Active sales pipeline (deals in flight, expected close dates)

### Section 4: Operations

- USPS-CMRA license + status
- Form 1583 archive (sample — full archive shown only after due
  diligence agreement)
- Quarterly USPS reporting (last 8 quarters)
- Vendor list (Stripe, USPS, carriers, software, etc.)
- Lease agreements (storefront, office)
- Insurance policies + coverage limits
- Employee list (W-2 + 1099 contractors)
- Employment agreements (signed)
- Vendor contracts (signed)
- Customer contracts (sample for each product line)
- Privacy policy + Terms of service (current versions)
- Compliance certifications (USPS-CMRA, etc.)

### Section 5: Product + technology

- Software architecture overview
- Source code repository access (provided after NDA only)
- Technology stack documentation
- Hosting / infrastructure costs breakdown
- Engineering team background
- Roadmap (last 12 months delivered + next 12 months planned)
- Open issues / bug tracker (Jira, Linear, etc.)
- Security posture (MFA, access controls, audit logs)
- Backups + disaster recovery procedures

### Section 6: Marketing + brand

- Brand guidelines (logo, colors, typography)
- Marketing assets library
- Content library (blog posts, videos, templates)
- SEO performance (Google Search Console data)
- Email marketing performance (Resend / Mailchimp data)
- Paid ads performance (Google Ads, Meta if applicable)
- Affiliate program performance
- Press coverage history

### Section 7: Legal

- Trademark filings (if any)
- IP assignments (employees + contractors)
- Any pending or past litigation
- Any regulatory complaints (USPS, BBB, state AG)
- Any customer disputes / refunds / chargebacks
- Privacy compliance (CCPA records, etc.)

### Section 8: Strategic

- Annual plans (sales kit doc 34) — last 3 years
- Quarterly review memos
- Key customer interview notes
- Competitor analysis (you have these in `/sales-kit/_competitor-pages.ts`)
- Market sizing (TAM / SAM / SOM)
- Growth projections (3-year + 5-year forecasts)

## Pitch deck (for investor / acquirer pitches)

A standard 10-15 slide deck:

### Slide 1: Title
- Company name + logo
- Founder name + tagline
- Date

### Slide 2: The problem
- Specific pain point (CMRA operators paying 20-40% to networks +
  small businesses needing real addresses)
- Quantify the problem (TAM = X)

### Slide 3: The solution
- What we do (CMRA + same-day delivery + business launch + SaaS)
- 1-sentence positioning

### Slide 4: Market opportunity
- Market size for CMRA industry: $X
- Adjacent markets: small business services ($Y), local courier
  ($Z)
- Total accessible market: $W

### Slide 5: Product
- Screenshots of: storefront, customer dashboard, admin dashboard,
  dispatch console
- Key features that differentiate

### Slide 6: Traction
- MRR growth chart (last 24 months)
- Customer count growth
- Net new customers / month
- Top customer logos (with permission)

### Slide 7: Business model
- 5 revenue lines
- Per-customer LTV by line
- Margin profile

### Slide 8: Go-to-market
- Channels we use (organic SEO, paid ads, LinkedIn outbound,
  affiliate, referral)
- Cost per acquisition by channel
- Conversion funnels

### Slide 9: Competition
- Competitive matrix (who&apos;s out there, what we do better)
- Why we win in our segment

### Slide 10: Team
- Founder background
- Key hires (if any)
- Advisors (if any)

### Slide 11: Financials
- Revenue + EBITDA by year (actual + forecast)
- Path to $5M MRR
- Unit economics

### Slide 12: Use of funds (raise scenarios) OR transaction structure (acquisition)
- For raises: how the new capital gets deployed (sales / engineering
  / operations breakdown)
- For acquisition: synergies the buyer gets

### Slide 13: Ask
- Round size (raise) OR valuation (acquisition)
- Closing timeline
- Any conditions

### Slide 14: Appendix
- Detailed financials
- Team bios
- Customer references

## What VCs actually look for

### Required for Series A+ ($5M-$15M raise):

- $1M+ ARR
- 30%+ YoY growth
- Net revenue retention (NRR) > 100%
- LTV/CAC > 3
- Less than 10% concentration in top customer
- Clear path to $10M ARR within 18-24 months

### What kills VC interest:

- Revenue concentration (one big customer = scary)
- High churn (over 5%/year for SaaS)
- Founder-dependent revenue (i.e., founder personally closes most
  deals)
- Bookkeeping mess
- Unclear unit economics
- "Growth at all costs" without margin discipline

For a CMRA-services-SaaS hybrid like ours, VCs are LESS likely to
fund (services component reduces SaaS multiple). PE / strategic
buyers are more likely to value the business correctly.

---

## What strategic acquirers value

Strategic acquirers in our space (iPostal1, Anytime Mailbox, postal
holdings) typically pay:

- 1.5-3x annual revenue (services component)
- 4-8x annual revenue OR 12-20x EBITDA (SaaS component)

So a $5M ARR business with 50/50 SaaS/services split could be valued
at:
- Services half ($2.5M ARR × 2x) = $5M
- SaaS half ($2.5M ARR × 6x) = $15M
- Combined: ~$20M

For a $60M ARR business: same multiples → maybe $200-300M.

The exact multiple depends on growth, retention, profitability,
strategic fit.

## Pre-acquisition operational hygiene

Things to clean up at least 12 months before any potential exit:

1. **Customer contracts**: ensure all are signed + valid
2. **Employee agreements**: ensure all signed + IP assignments in
   place
3. **Vendor contracts**: review for any change-of-control clauses
4. **Books**: ensure clean reconciliation (sales kit doc 43)
5. **Legal**: resolve any pending disputes
6. **Compliance**: USPS-CMRA license current, audits passed
7. **Tech debt**: pay down major tech debt that buyers will catch
8. **Documentation**: SOPs documented, keys to systems organized

A messy company is worth 30-50% less than a clean company at the
same revenue. Investing 6-12 months in cleanup pays off massively.

## Common transaction structures

### 1. Equity raise (no exit, dilution)

- VC / PE invests cash for X% of equity
- Founder retains operational control
- Returns realized only at later exit / IPO
- Typical for high-growth SaaS

### 2. Acquisition / merger (full exit)

- Buyer pays cash + stock
- Founder usually has 1-2 year earnout (must hit metrics)
- Founder transitions out of operational role over 6-24 months
- Most common exit for service businesses

### 3. Recapitalization (partial exit + new partner)

- Founder sells 30-70% to new investor (often PE)
- Cash to founder
- Founder retains some equity + operating role
- Common for businesses growing past founder&apos;s capacity

### 4. Earnout deal

- Buyer pays X now + Y if business hits Z metric
- Useful when buyer + seller disagree on future value
- Risk for seller: future may not materialize as planned

### 5. Asset sale (vs stock sale)

- Buyer purchases specific assets / customer contracts
- Seller keeps liabilities
- Tax-different from stock sale (higher tax for seller; lower for buyer)
- Common for service businesses

## Negotiation principles

### Don&apos;t negotiate alone

Hire an investment banker or M&A advisor for any transaction over
$5M. Their fee (1-3% of transaction value) is recouped by the
better terms they negotiate.

### Don&apos;t fall in love with one buyer

Run a process with multiple parties. Even 2-3 competing offers
significantly improves terms.

### Term sheet ≠ closing

A signed term sheet (LOI) is a non-binding agreement. Closing
involves due diligence, definitive agreement, and final closing
docs. Expect 60-180 days from LOI to close.

### Watch out for

- Earnouts where the buyer controls the metrics
- Working-capital adjustments (can shift hundreds of thousands)
- Indemnification caps (your liability after close)
- Non-compete provisions (how long, what scope)
- Rep + warranty insurance options

### Counter-intuitive negotiation move

Sometimes the best move is to walk away. If a buyer is taking advantage
of urgency, willingness to walk improves terms more than concessions.

---

## What this playbook ISN&apos;T

- Not legal advice. Hire an M&A attorney for any actual transaction.
- Not financial advice. Hire an investment banker for transactions
  over $5M.
- Not for revenue under $500k. At very small scale, this is overkill.

## When you DON&apos;T want to raise / sell

Most bootstrapped business owners don&apos;t want / need either.
Reasons to avoid:

- You like running the business + control matters more than cash
- The business is profitable + cash-flowing — no need for capital
- Investors / acquirers would change the culture / customer focus
- You&apos;d rather pass to family / employees / heirs over time

There&apos;s no obligation to ever exit. A profitable bootstrap
business compounding at 30-50% YoY is a fine outcome. A multi-
generation family business is a fine outcome.

This playbook is for IF you want to raise / sell. Not WHEN you must.

---

## The big idea

Investor / acquisition prep isn&apos;t something you do at the
deal. It&apos;s something you do every quarter, every year, of
operating the business.

Clean financials. Clean contracts. Clean operations. Clean
documentation.

When the moment comes — raise, sell, recapitalization — you&apos;ll
be ready in days, not months. The 12-month panic-cleanup is what
costs founders 30%+ on valuation.

Build clean. Operate clean. The optionality compounds.
