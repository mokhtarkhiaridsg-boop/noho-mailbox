# Pricing Objection Handler

Every common pricing objection we hear, with concrete responses.
Organized by product line.

**Principle**: don&apos;t discount. Discounts cheapen the product +
set bad precedents. Reframe to value instead.

---

## Mailbox Plans ($50-$295 / 3 months)

### "Your plans are more expensive than [iPostal1 / UPS Store]"

```
"You&apos;re right that some virtual mailbox plans start at $10-$15/mo.
What&apos;s usually different at the lower-priced tiers:

- Per-piece scanning fees ($1-$3 each, adds up fast for active mail)
- Forwarding markups (25-50% on postage)
- No notary on Form 1583 ($25 added cost)
- No walk-in counter

Our $50/3-month Basic tier ($16.67/mo) includes 25 free scans, free
notary on Business+, and a real walk-in counter. So apples-to-apples,
the math is closer than the headline price.

If your use case is purely virtual address with under 5 pieces of mail
per month, iPostal1 might be cheaper. If you&apos;re using the address
actively, we usually come out ahead."
```

### "I can use a P.O. Box for $10/month at the post office"

```
"P.O. Boxes work for general personal mail, but they fail in three
common scenarios:

1. Etsy / Amazon / Stripe verification — they reject P.O. Boxes
2. Bank account opening — most banks won&apos;t accept P.O. Box for
   business accounts
3. LLC formation in California — CA Secretary of State rejects P.O.
   Boxes

If you&apos;re doing any of those, you need a real CMRA address
specifically. P.O. Box and CMRA address aren&apos;t equivalent legally.

If you&apos;re just doing personal mail forwarding, P.O. Box is fine
and we&apos;d say go for it."
```

### "$50 for 3 months is more than I want to commit"

```
"Reasonable. Two options:

1. Sign up for Basic ($50/3 mo). If you cancel before the next
   billing cycle, you&apos;re only out $50 — same as one month of
   most other services.

2. Wait until you have a specific need (Etsy verification, LLC
   filing, banking) — then sign up. The plan starts when you sign
   up, so no rush.

Most customers who hesitate at $50 end up signing up within 60-90
days when something forces the issue. We&apos;ll be here either way."
```

### "What if I want to cancel?"

```
"Cancel anytime. We don&apos;t prorate refunds for partial periods —
your service runs through whatever you&apos;ve paid for. So if
you&apos;ve paid for 3 months, you have 3 months. Then it stops.

No cancellation fee. No questions asked. We don&apos;t make you call
to cancel — you can do it in your dashboard."
```

## $2,000 Business Launch Bundle

### "$2,000 is a lot for an LLC"

```
"You&apos;re right that the LLC filing itself is only $70 in California.
You can absolutely DIY for ~$890 first year (filing + Statement of
Information + first year&apos;s franchise tax) plus your time.

What the $2k bundle includes that DIY doesn&apos;t:

- LLC filing (we do the paperwork)
- EIN application
- Operating Agreement (custom for your business)
- Brand kit: logo, color palette, typography by a real designer
- 5-page custom website (not a template)
- 12 months of mail at our LA address
- Free notarized Form 1583 (saves $25)
- Real human owning your engagement start to finish

Most piecemeal services (LegalZoom + Squarespace + a designer + a
mailbox + notary) run $4-6k for the same components.

If you don&apos;t need the brand kit + website, our mailbox plans are
$50-$295 / 3 months. If you do need them, $2k is the cheapest path."
```

### "Can you do half the bundle for $1k?"

```
"No — we&apos;d lose money. The bundle is priced as a package because
the components only make economic sense bundled. The brand designer
charges $500-$1500 just for the brand kit. The website builder charges
$1000+ just for the 5-page site. We can offer it at $2k flat because
we coordinate it.

If you only need part of it, here&apos;s the unbundled split:

- LLC formation alone: $890 first year if you DIY, ~$1,500 with a
  service like LegalZoom
- Brand kit alone: $500-$1500 from a designer
- 5-page website alone: $1,000-$3,000 custom, $90+/mo for a builder
- Mailbox alone: $50/3 months

Add them up — same $2-3k. We&apos;re trying to consolidate, not
discount."
```

### "LegalZoom is cheaper"

```
"LegalZoom&apos;s starter LLC is $0 + state fee. By the time you add
their EIN ($70), Operating Agreement ($199 in their Premier tier),
and registered agent ($249 first year), you&apos;re at ~$520. That
covers LLC formation only.

You still need:
- Brand kit (separate vendor — LegalZoom doesn&apos;t do this)
- Website (separate vendor — LegalZoom does have a basic site builder
  for $90+/mo)
- Mailbox / address (separate vendor)
- Notarized Form 1583 (separate cost, ~$25)

By the time you&apos;ve coordinated all of those, you&apos;re at
$2,000-$3,500. We bundle for $2,000 flat with one point of contact.

If your only need is LLC formation, LegalZoom works. If you need the
whole launch, we save you 1-2k and a lot of vendor coordination."
```

### "I&apos;m not sure I need an LLC yet"

```
"If you&apos;re not sure, you probably don&apos;t. The $800/yr
California franchise tax is real money — paying it for liability
protection you don&apos;t need yet is wasteful.

Two ways to figure out if it&apos;s worth it:

1. Take our 9-question free quiz: nohomailbox.org/tools/should-i-form-an-llc
   It&apos;s honest — about 1 in 5 people get told &apos;don&apos;t
   form one yet.&apos;

2. Reply to me with what your business is + revenue + risk profile,
   and I&apos;ll give you a real opinion. About 20% of the leads I
   see get told to wait."
```

## CMRA SaaS ($299-$1,499 / month)

### "$299 is more than what I&apos;m paying iPostal1"

```
"What&apos;s your iPostal1 cost today, exactly? In our experience
operators paying iPostal1 for 50+ boxes are paying $200-$400/mo in
combined subscription rev share + per-piece fees + forwarding
markup. So $299 flat is usually equal or less.

The math gets dramatically better at scale because our license stays
$299 flat while iPostal1&apos;s take grows with your revenue.

Would you reply with your active box count + your average monthly
total to iPostal1? I&apos;ll send a custom comparison."
```

### "Can you do less than $299?"

```
"$299 is our floor. Below that the math doesn&apos;t work for us — we
fund engineering, support, hosting, USPS-CMRA compliance work.

If $299 is a stretch, the right answer is probably &apos;wait a quarter
or two until you&apos;re ready.&apos; Migration takes effort and you
want to be sure the platform fee is sustainable.

Our smallest active operator is at 35 boxes. They&apos;re close to
break-even on the license. They&apos;re scaling fast — if you&apos;re
NOT scaling, the platform fee won&apos;t feel right."
```

### "What about a free trial?"

```
"We don&apos;t do free trials on the SaaS — partly because migration
is real work for both sides, and partly because operators who haven&apos;t
committed don&apos;t actually evaluate the platform seriously.

What we DO offer:

1. Real demo (30 minutes) — see the actual admin, member portal,
   dispatch, billing, compliance modules
2. Reference call with current operator using the platform
3. Migration playbook (free download) — see what the migration actually
   looks like before committing

If you want to start, the first 30 days are at-risk for both of us.
You can cancel any time. We don&apos;t lock you in."
```

### "What if the software doesn&apos;t do [feature X]?"

```
"Honest answer: tell me what feature, and I&apos;ll either:

- Show you it (we might already have it)
- Add it to the roadmap with a realistic timeline
- Tell you it&apos;s not on roadmap so you can make an informed call

We don&apos;t fake features. About 50% of operator-requested features
ship within 90 days because they&apos;re universal. About 30% take
longer or sit on the roadmap. About 20% we politely decline because
they&apos;re too niche or conflict with our overall design.

What&apos;s the feature?"
```

## Same-Day Delivery ($5-$24 per run)

### "DoorDash / Uber Connect is cheaper"

```
"Sometimes — but compare like-for-like:

DoorDash for a 5-mile run might quote $8-15. We&apos;re $9.75 flat for
LA-wide except WeHo/Westside ($24).

Difference:
- Surge pricing: DoorDash flexes 1.5-2x at peak. We don&apos;t.
- Driver continuity: DoorDash uses any driver. We have repeat drivers
  who learn your business.
- Wait time: DoorDash drivers don&apos;t wait. Ours do (within reason).
- Receipt + signature: DoorDash drivers don&apos;t sign or document.
  Ours give photo + signature confirmation.
- B2B reliability: DoorDash drivers cancel on you. Ours don&apos;t.

For one-time runs, DoorDash works. For B2B regulars (court runs, lab
work, real estate, recurring), we&apos;re cheaper net of the chaos
costs."
```

### "We have our own internal courier"

```
"That works. The math we usually run with operators considering this:

Internal courier loaded cost (W-2 driver + vehicle + insurance + PTO)
is typically $40-60/hour. So a 30-minute run = $20-30.

Our fee for the same run = $9.75. You free up your driver for higher-
value tasks (or staff savings).

If your internal courier is at 80%+ utilization, keeping them is
fine. If they have idle time, outsourcing one-off runs to us frees
that capacity. We do this for 6 customers who keep an internal driver
+ use us for overflow."
```

### "Can you do recurring routes for less per run?"

```
"Yes — if you commit to 5+ runs per week at the same time, we discount
to per-run pricing on a multi-week schedule:

- 5-9 runs/week: 10% off per run
- 10-19 runs/week: 15% off per run
- 20+ runs/week: 20% off per run

Plus same driver every week (reliability) and predictable scheduling
(no surge / no surprise).

Usually pays for itself within 4-6 weeks vs ad-hoc booking."
```

## Cross-product objections

### "I need to think about it / talk to my partner"

```
"Of course. Two things to make that easier:

1. I&apos;ll send a one-page summary of what we discussed by email. If
   you forward it to your partner, the highlights are right there.

2. Ping me back when you&apos;ve decided. No follow-up nag-emails —
   one or two friendly reminders, then I move on.

What&apos;s your timeline? I want to make sure I follow up at the
right time."
```

### "Send me an invoice and I&apos;ll think about it"

```
"Happy to. Quick clarification — invoices imply you&apos;ve decided to
buy. If you&apos;re still evaluating, I&apos;d send a quote (no
commitment) instead. Want that, or are you ready to commit?

Either way, I&apos;ll send what you need within an hour."
```

### "Your competitor [X] told me they could do it for less"

```
"What did they quote you and what&apos;s included?

Couple things to verify:
1. Same scope (apples-to-apples)
2. Same timeline
3. Same point of accountability (one person owning the relationship,
   or a help-desk queue)

If they really are cheaper for the same scope + timeline +
accountability, they win and you should go with them. We&apos;re not
the cheapest — we&apos;re consistent.

If the comparison isn&apos;t apples-to-apples (which it usually
isn&apos;t when prices differ a lot), I can show you what the gap
actually is."
```

### "I&apos;ll get back to you next week"

```
"Sounds good. Calendar reminder: I&apos;ll follow up on [next-week-date]
to see where you&apos;re at. If you&apos;re ready earlier, just reply.

If something specific would help your decision (a customer reference,
a custom proposal, a demo), let me know now and I&apos;ll prep it.

Anything else you need before then?"
```

## When to actually walk away

There ARE prospects you should not chase. Walk away if:

1. They demand 50%+ discount and the math clearly doesn&apos;t work.
   Discounted customers churn faster + complain more.
2. They&apos;re shopping you against 5+ competitors and using all of
   you for free advice. Race to the bottom.
3. Their use case is fundamentally wrong for our product (e.g., they
   need international shipping, we&apos;re US-only).
4. Red flags: aggressive language, scope creep before signing,
   asking for things outside our model.

How to walk away well:
```
"Based on what you&apos;ve described, I think [competitor / different
solution] is probably a better fit for you. We&apos;re not trying to
take customers we can&apos;t serve well.

If your needs change in the future, reach out. We&apos;ll be here.
Best of luck."
```

This is fine — and customers who hear this often respect it and come
back later.

## The big idea

Pricing objections are usually proxy for risk concerns. The customer
isn&apos;t saying &apos;you&apos;re too expensive&apos; literally —
they&apos;re saying &apos;I&apos;m not yet convinced this is worth
$X.&apos;

The fix isn&apos;t lowering price. It&apos;s building enough trust
that the price question evaporates.

Tools for trust-building:
- Reference customer call
- Case study with real numbers
- 30-day risk-free start (cancel anytime, no fees)
- Written guarantee on specific deliverables

Use those before discounting.
