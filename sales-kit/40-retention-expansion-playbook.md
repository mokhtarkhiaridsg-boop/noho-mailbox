# Retention + Expansion Playbook

After onboarding (doc 39), the long-term game is keeping customers +
growing their account size. Retention is cheaper than acquisition;
expansion within an existing account is the cheapest revenue you can
ever get.

**Principle**: Customers with multiple touchpoints + multiple products
churn at half the rate of single-product customers. Every interaction
should be a chance to build on the relationship.

---

## Retention math

Why retention matters more than acquisition:

| Scenario | Year 1 LTV | Year 3 LTV | Year 5 LTV |
|---|---|---|---|
| 5%/mo churn | $480 (avg $40 × 12mo) | ~$520 | ~$525 |
| 2%/mo churn | $480 | ~$960 | ~$1,400 |
| 1%/mo churn | $480 | ~$1,300 | ~$2,200 |

A single percentage point of churn reduction can double LTV over 3-5
years. Retention is leverage.

For our scale of business:
- Mailbox plans at $50/3mo. 5%/mo churn = avg lifetime ~20 months,
  LTV ~$330. 2%/mo churn = ~50 months avg lifetime, LTV ~$830.
- $2k Bundle: one-time, but ~30% become recurring mailbox customers.
  Of those, retention math kicks in.
- SaaS: $300-$1,500/mo. 2%/mo churn = ~50-month avg lifetime. At
  $700/mo average, LTV = $35,000.

## Mailbox plan retention

### Quarterly renewal moments

Plans bill every 3 months. So every 90 days, the customer makes a
"do I keep this?" decision implicitly. Maximize the quarterly
renewal:

**60 days before renewal**:
- Email summary: "you&apos;ve received [X] pieces of mail, scanned
  [Y] documents, forwarded [Z] packages this quarter"
- Soft-pitch upgrade if usage warrants (e.g., they hit the 25-scan
  free tier limit 2 months in a row → suggest Premium)

**30 days before renewal**:
- Auto-renew reminder (CAN-SPAM compliant)
- Personal email if they&apos;re a Premium / Business customer

**At renewal**:
- Auto-charge (per terms of service)
- "Thanks for renewing" email with a preview of new features
  shipped this quarter

**If they cancel**:
- Exit survey: 1 question. "What could we have done differently?"
- Reply within 1 hour with personal email.
- If the reason is fixable, offer a fix + 30-day extension at no
  charge. About 40% take this.

### Annual reviews (year 1, year 2, year 3+)

Yearly anniversary email:
- "You&apos;ve been a customer for [N] years"
- Specific stats: total mail received, total scans, total forwards
- Loyalty offer: "as a thank-you, here&apos;s [discount / upgrade
  / free notary / etc.]"

These build emotional retention beyond the rational "is this still
worth it?" check.

## $2k Bundle retention (cross-sell to mailbox)

90% of bundle customers become recurring mailbox customers (the
12-month mailbox is included in the bundle). Retention focus:

**Month 11** (1 month before mailbox auto-renewal):
- "Your year of mail is almost up — here&apos;s what we recommend
  for ongoing service"
- Offer 10% off renewal as bundle alumni
- Highlight any new features shipped since their bundle delivery

**Year-1 anniversary**:
- Big-picture review: what they accomplished in year 1
- Upsell to Brand Retainer ($1,200/mo) for ongoing brand work if
  applicable
- Soft pitch: "as you grow, what do you need next?"

## CMRA SaaS retention (highest LTV, requires highest investment)

### Quarterly business review (QBR)

For every active SaaS customer, schedule a 60-minute QBR every 90
days.

**QBR agenda**:
1. Review their numbers (active customers, MRR, churn, mail volume)
   over the last quarter
2. Identify wins + challenges
3. Roadmap update (what we shipped last quarter, what&apos;s coming)
4. Specific feature requests they have
5. Their growth plans (any hiring? location expansion? new product
   lines?)
6. Renewal confirmation OR pricing-tier review

**Output**: 1-page memo with action items for both sides. Sent
within 24 hours.

### Quarterly retention math

CMRA SaaS customers who:
- Have a QBR every 90 days: <5% annual churn
- Don&apos;t have QBRs: 25-40% annual churn

QBRs feel like overhead but they're the cheapest churn reduction
move available.

### Health-score watching

Score every SaaS customer monthly on:
- Active customer count change (growing = healthy, shrinking = at
  risk)
- Login frequency (admin staff using daily = healthy)
- Support ticket tone (collaborative = healthy, frustrated = risk)
- Payment history (always on time = healthy, recent failures = risk)

Customers scoring poorly on 2+ dimensions get extra attention.
Founder-level intervention is appropriate at this stage.

## Same-day delivery retention (B2B regulars)

The retention game here is different from subscription products
because each transaction is discrete.

### Build the habit

For new B2B regulars:
- After 5 runs in their first month, send a "set up recurring routes"
  pitch
- Recurring-route customers churn at 1/4 the rate of ad-hoc customers

For existing recurring customers:
- Monthly invoice email also includes a usage summary
- Quarterly check-in: "we noticed you booked 32 runs this quarter.
  Anything we can do better?"

### Volume incentives

Encourage growth within the account:
- 5+ runs/week: 10% off per run
- 10+ runs/week: 15% off per run
- 20+ runs/week: 20% off per run + dedicated driver assignment

## Affiliate / partner retention

### Top affiliates (>$2k earned)

Quarterly call with each top affiliate:
- Review what&apos;s working / not in their channel
- Custom asset requests
- Co-branded landing page possibility
- Early access to new product launches

### Mid-tier affiliates ($500-$2k earned)

Quarterly email check-in (no call):
- Performance review
- Asset suggestions
- "What can we make easier?"

### Long-tail affiliates (<$500 earned)

Annual email:
- Performance summary
- Reminder of asset pack
- "If you want a custom copy review, reply"

Most affiliate churn happens at the long-tail. They sign up,
forget, never return. That&apos;s OK — focus retention on the top
20% who drive 80% of revenue.

## Expansion (selling more to existing customers)

### Cross-sell paths

**Mailbox → $2k Bundle**:
- Trigger: customer asks about LLC formation
- Or: customer&apos;s mailbox volume suggests an active business
- Or: anniversary-year communication

**Mailbox → Same-day delivery**:
- Trigger: customer is in our delivery zone
- Or: they ask about getting a package picked up
- Or: they mention a business that does courier work

**$2k Bundle → Brand Retainer ($1,200/mo)**:
- Trigger: customer&apos;s business is growing and brand needs
  iteration (logo update, new collateral, additional pages)
- Or: 6 months post-bundle delivery, customer&apos;s site is
  outdated

**Customer → Affiliate**:
- Trigger: customer mentions sharing the link with friends
- Or: customer has a YouTube / blog / podcast
- Or: customer success story emerges

**Mailbox → Premium upsell**:
- Trigger: customer hits 25-scan free tier limit 2+ months in a row
- Or: customer requests same-day delivery 2+ times a month
- Or: customer asks about international forwarding

### Upsell timing

Don&apos;t pitch upsells in the first 30 days (you&apos;re still
proving baseline value).

Best moments:
- 30-day check-in (light mention)
- 90-day review (moderate pitch with data)
- 1-year anniversary (full upsell + loyalty discount)

Bad moments:
- During support tickets (customer is annoyed; not a sell moment)
- During cancel attempts (looks desperate)
- Within 7 days of any complaint

## Win-back campaigns (canceled customers)

### 30 days post-cancel

"Hey [Name] — saw you canceled. Reading back through your support
history, looks like [reason inferred from records]. Anything we can
do better if you ever come back?"

This is a real question, not a sales pitch. Listen. Take notes.
Improve the product based on patterns.

### 90 days post-cancel

"Hey [Name] — we&apos;ve shipped [list of new features] since you
canceled. If any of those address what didn&apos;t fit before,
we&apos;d love to have you back. 10% off your first 3 months."

Most canceled customers don&apos;t come back. ~10-15% do, IF the
follow-up is sincere.

### 1-year post-cancel

If they haven&apos;t replied to either prior email, drop them.
Don&apos;t spam.

## Tracking retention

Per customer (in CRM / spreadsheet):
- Signup date
- Activation date (first value)
- Last login / last engagement
- Health score (per-product framework)
- Renewal status
- Cumulative spend
- Cancellation reason (if applicable)
- Win-back attempt status (if canceled)

Weekly aggregate:
- Active customers
- New customers this week
- Cancellations this week
- Net new
- Customer-weighted churn rate (% of dollar revenue lost / % of
  customer count)

Monthly aggregate:
- Cohort analysis: customers who signed up in [month X] — what %
  are still active?
- LTV trend: average LTV across all active customers
- Top expansion customers: who upgraded / cross-sold this month?

## Anti-patterns

1. **Annual contracts to lock in customers.** Lock-in feels like
   retention but it&apos;s a substitute. Customers who genuinely
   want to be there don&apos;t need a lock-in.
2. **Cancellation friction.** Hiding the cancel button. Forcing
   phone calls to cancel. Discount surprises at cancel. All make
   customers angrier, hurt brand, increase one-star reviews.
3. **Discounts without a story.** "Here&apos;s 10% off if you stay"
   teaches customers to threaten cancellation for discounts. Better:
   "We shipped X new features for you, here&apos;s a one-time loyalty
   credit."
4. **Upsells on every email.** Cross-sell timing matters. Bombarding
   = unsubscribe.
5. **Treating customers as numbers.** When a customer churns, a real
   person made a real decision. Reach out personally.

---

## What this playbook is NOT

- Not a substitute for a great product. If your product is bad,
  retention is impossible.
- Not a replacement for talking to customers. Quarterly calls beat
  any retention email sequence.
- Not zero-sum with acquisition. Both matter — but retention is the
  multiplier on acquisition.

---

## The big idea

Acquisition is sprinting. Retention is breathing. You can&apos;t
sprint forever. The business that wins long-term is one with
acquisition that&apos;s good enough + retention that&apos;s
exceptional.

Optimize for: customers who would tell their friends about you.
Those customers stay long, spend more, and bring in more customers.
Everything else is downstream of that.
